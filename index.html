Overview

Archean oceans are significant to the development of both the Earth, and the evolution of life itself. This period of time is so far into the past that it is often difficult to ascertain the exact conditions that existed. However, this is not to say that it is impossible to gain an understanding of what this environment was like. In fact, surviving rocks from this eon provide clues as to what these areas were like. Further, micro-fossils and stromatolites exist which can help identify early organisms which likely existed during this time9.

Conditions

The Earth during the Archean eon was quite different from what we are accustomed to today. For one thing, species who rely on oxygen (like us) would find the environment most hostile. This is because the atmosphere was not anywhere near as oxyginated as it is today. In fact, if you were to go back in time to the Archean, you would be more likely to be breathing toxic gases such as methane or ammonia than you would oxygen 1. Still, the early ocean environment of this time in Earth's history provided the opportunity for life to spring forth.

In addition to the oxygen-poor environment, ocean temperature and volume was also much higher compared to modern day. Estimates of the temperature of the oceans at the time can even go as high as 167oF or 185oF2! It is possible that higher temperatures were maintained in part due to the composition of the atmosphere at the time, particularly an abundance in greenhouse gases3. In a similar extreme, it is speculated that ocean volume could have been as much as twice what it is today3. A more familiar feature of the Archean oceans may be hydrothermal vents. These vents have been frequently proposed as likely candidates for early habitats of life4.

Some of the oldest indications of life come from the Archeon eon of Earth's history, with evidence from the ocean being particularly important. Notably, there are traces of life which can be found in the form of micro-fossils1. Fossils such as these go back as far as 3.5 billion years in the past1. However, there is potential evidence that is even older. There is some indication that Graphite found in rocks that are over 3.7 billion years old may have been the result of biological activity 5. This highlights the importance of this time, in relevance to the emergence of life on Earth.

While there exist several hypotheses as to the origin of life on Earth, a particularly intriguing proposal is that it arose around hydrothermal vents34. Essentially, the oceans of the early Archean would have seen hydrothermal vents which provided an environment that was highly alkaline, with significant levels of Hydrogen3. It is proposed that environments like these could have been important to the emergence of life4. This further underscores why the Earth's early oceans are fascinating, they were likely home to biology at its youngest age.

A no less important piece to the puzzle that is early life, in marine environments, comes in the form of stromatolites6. In order to make it clear why that is, it is vital to discuss cyanobacteria. Essentially, cyanobacteria are a type of bacteria which dwell in marine environments and survive via photosynthesis7. As previously mentioned, the atmosphere of the Archeon was not oxyginated, like it is today. As such, photosynthesis from cyanobacterial colonies would deplete carbon dioxide from nearby water, leading to the precipitation of calcium carbonate8. Finally, these precipitations formed distinctive layers which can be preserved, providing evidence for the organism's presence8.

Still, it is important to note that obtaining clear evidence of life from this period of time is very difficult. In fact, there is some indication that anoxygenic photosynthetic bacteria could also have produced stromatolites6. Essentially, it is not a simple matter to attribute what is observed to a particular organism or process. In addition, definitive micro-fossils are quite rare. Some of the more clear fossils from the oceans of the Archean come in the form of microscopic speroidal structures produced from hydrothermal activity6. More specifically, structures from hydrothermal activity, found in chert, can be compared to similar organic structures from the Proterozoic in search of correlation6. Though the evidence for life during this time is not as cut-and-dry as fossilized bones, it does still exist.

Beyond

While the oceans of the Archean may have been quite different to what is observed today, nothing would be same without this significant environment. Perhaps most important, is the development of life in these ancient waters. The reason for this is the development of photosynthesis in cyanobacteria. It is argued that the arrival of oxygenic photosynthesis may have come during the late Archean, thus providing a vital source of oxygen for the future atmosphere of the Earth3. It is therefore of endless interest to the people of today to decipher exactly what the conditions were like which led to this development, and how they may have directly influenced other aspects of Earth as it is now.
